Scroll through any "AI for writers" search and you'll find the same video eleven times: a list of prompts, delivered with the energy of a magic trick. Copy this. Paste that. Watch the words appear. It's not wrong, exactly — it's just incomplete. A prompt is one move in a much longer game, and if you only ever learn the move, you'll keep reaching for it in situations it was never built for.

What actually changes a writer's output isn't a better prompt. It's a better **pipeline** — a repeatable path from "I have a topic" to "I have a finished, well-sourced draft," where each tool hands off cleanly to the next one instead of fighting for the same job.

## The pipeline, in order

Here's the actual sequence, stripped of any tool-worship. This is what runs before a single word of the article gets written.

1. **[[Searching]]** — Start wider than Google. Depending on the topic, that means a proper database or search engine built for the subject, not just whatever ranks first.
2. **[[Research]]** — Everything gets pulled into NotebookLM as the hub. This is the step most people skip — they read fifteen tabs and try to hold the connections in their head instead of letting a tool do it.
3. **[[Data Analysis]]** — Before writing anything, ask the research what it's actually saying. What repeats? What contradicts? What's the one claim every source agrees on?
4. **[[Second Brain]]** — Findings move into Obsidian as structured, linked notes — not a wall of text, but a set of ideas you can rearrange like index cards.
5. **[[Prompting]]** — Only now does an AI model touch the draft, and by this point the prompt is short, because the thinking already happened.

> The prompt is the last five percent of the work. Everything that makes it good happened before you typed it.

## Why order matters more than tools

Swap NotebookLM for a different research tool and this pipeline still works. Swap Claude for another model and it still works. The tools are replaceable; the **order** is not. Writers who skip straight to "prompt → draft" are optimizing the one step that was never the bottleneck. Research depth was always the bottleneck — it's just faster to close now.

This is the whole premise of the10XWriter: not "here's a tool," but "here's where it sits in the chain, and here's what happens if you get the order wrong." Every video and article here maps back to one link in this pipeline.
